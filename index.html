Did you know? The United States has the highest maternal mortality rate of any other developed nation — 3 to 7 times that of countries like Finland, France, Sweden, Portugal, and the U.K. — and we are the only developed nation where that rate is on the rise.10 A disproportionate lack of research, funding and training nationally on maternal issues9 suggests a systemic bias. The illegality of abortion in the United States was rather short lived, beginning in the mid-1800s and ending with Roe v. Wade in 1973. The ongoing denouncement in the Catholic Church also began relatively recently, in the mid-1800s (though the Pope's recent admissions of obtaining abortions for nuns raped by priests might put the validity of this position into question).39 Prior to that, abortion has a long documented history extending over 4000 years.1 Termination of pregnancy is considered a human right by the United Nations, and lack of access to it is considered to be torture in cases of rape, incest, and risk of death for the mother.25

What makes fewer abortions? It’s not exposure to the church; 62% of people who have abortions nationally are religious, 54% are either Protestant, evangelical Protestant, or Catholic,5 and some even picket clinics themselves.35 It’s not laws; internationally, rates of abortion are near equal in countries with the most extreme restrictions and in those with the most lenient, even negligibly higher in the former.24 It’s not shaming, stigma, threat of jail, or illness or death resulting from unsafe procedures.29 What does reduce abortion? Sexual education and access to contraception is proven to lower rates.20,33 One example, a program in Colorado that gave out free IUDs to women that wanted them, turned around a 35% drop in abortion rate and saved the public $80M in Medicaid costs, but the program was cut by Republican lawmakers due to pressure from Christian groups.34

In countries and states where the practice is banned or prevented, what happens? Maternal mortality and incarceration rates increase. Maternal mortality is 8-11% higher22,23 in states with extreme abortion restrictions like Louisiana, Georgia, and Texas,22 and in countries with the most extremely restrictive policies, including Iraq, Honduras, and El Slavador (With our country's long history with Afghanistan, and talking points surrounding its attitudes towards women, I feel the need to mention that the practice is even allowed there, at least to save a woman's life).7 Some possible reasons for increased maternal mortality include complications from unsafe abortions, suicide, and doctor refusal to prescribe lifesaving medicine or surgery in case of risk to the fetus, such as in ectopic pregnancy.6 More women are in jail in these states and countries, for abortion, but also for natural miscarriage wrongfully charged as abortion.23
